Rain tapped the fire escape like nervous fingers. The city wore its midnight coat, all wet brick and yellow windows.

Mara waited beneath the broken theater sign. Every few seconds, the neon buzzed awake, painted her face red, then died again.

The envelope in her pocket felt heavier than paper had any right to feel. Names, dates, numbers, secrets. Enough truth to ruin a mayor, or save a ghost.

Across the street, a taxi idled with its lights off. Nobody sat in the driver's seat. That was the first bad sign.

The second was the man in the gray hat, reflected in the pawnshop glass. He smiled when she noticed him.

Mara did not run. Running made noise. Instead, she stepped into the alley, counted three heartbeats, and let the darkness close behind her.

Some nights the city whispered. Tonight, it sharpened its teeth.
